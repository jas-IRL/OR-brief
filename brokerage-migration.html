Influencing Variables

Other things happening at the same time that could move the same metrics. If a number shifts after launch, check these first.

Tax Season (Feb–Apr)
Every year, brokerage contact volume spikes during tax season as customers deal with 1099s, cost basis questions, and tax-related account changes. This happens regardless of the migration.
Why it matters: If resolution rate drops or handle time increases in March–April, it could be the volume spike — not the migration. We need to compare against the same period last year, not just the pre-launch baseline.
FE Advocate Tenure Ramp
FE's 11 Specialists started in November 2025. By launch, they'll have 5–7 months of experience. New advocates naturally improve over time — faster resolution, fewer transfers, better QA scores.
Why it matters: If metrics improve after launch, it could be the advocates getting better at their existing work — not the migration going well. We need to separate natural ramp from migration impact.
Internal Team Restructuring
If internal brokerage advocates are redeployed, reassigned, or reduced during the same window, their performance data changes independently of the migration.
Why it matters: A drop in internal team metrics could look like migration fallout when it's actually a staffing change. We need to track internal headcount and role changes alongside performance.
QA Rubric or Scoring Changes
If the QA team updates the rubric, changes how questions are weighted, or rotates auditors, QA scores will shift even if advocate behavior stays the same.
Why it matters: A QA score drop from 98 to 94 could mean advocates are struggling with new work — or it could mean the rubric got harder. We need to confirm no scoring changes happened during the measurement window.
Cash Investing Product Changes
New features, UI changes, or product updates to Cash Investing can shift what customers contact us about. A new feature launch could create a wave of unfamiliar contact types.
Why it matters: If FCR drops after launch, it could be because advocates are seeing new scenarios they haven't been trained on — driven by a product change, not the migration. We need to cross-reference product release dates.

Industry Worldview

How leading fintech companies approach BPO transitions for regulated work — patterns, benchmarks, and competitive intelligence.

Industry Patterns for Brokerage BPO Transitions

Tier 1/Tier 2 splits are standard practice across fintech brokerage operations. Robinhood, Webull, and SoFi all use BPO partners for routine account inquiries, trade confirmations, and basic troubleshooting (Tier 1), while keeping complex scenarios — regulatory escalations, account restrictions, fraud investigations — with internal teams (Tier 2). The split is driven by FINRA oversight requirements: BPO sites can handle licensed work, but firms typically retain direct control over scenarios with regulatory exposure or reputational risk.

The most common failure mode is undefined escalation paths. When BPO advocates don't have clear rules on what to escalate versus what to resolve independently, two things happen: (1) over-escalation creates bottlenecks at Tier 2, and (2) under-escalation leads to compliance violations when advocates handle scenarios beyond their training or authority. Stripe and Chime both experienced this during early BPO expansions — volume shifted successfully, but escalation queues spiked 3–4x because the boundary between Tier 1 and Tier 2 wasn't operationally clear.

Tool permission parity is non-negotiable for regulated work. If BPO advocates lack the same system access as internal teams, they create workarounds — copying data into external tools, escalating cases that should be resolvable, or making decisions without full context. This is both a compliance risk (FINRA expects consistent handling regardless of advocate location) and an operational risk (workarounds degrade quality and slow resolution). Coinbase and Kraken both mandate tool audits before any BPO scope expansion touching financial accounts.

What This Means for Cash App

Tier 1 readiness is table stakes. FE Orem's 98–100 QA scores and improving autonomy metrics put them in the top quartile of BPO brokerage performance. Proceeding with Tier 1 is low-risk and aligns with industry norms.

Tier 2 scope definition is the critical path. Every peer who successfully expanded BPO into Tier 2 work started with a documented escalation matrix that defined boundaries at the scenario level — not just "complex work stays internal." Without this, you inherit the same escalation bottleneck that Stripe and Chime experienced.

Tool parity is a compliance requirement, not a nice-to-have. FINRA expects consistent handling regardless of advocate location. If FE lacks the same system access as internal teams, you're creating a two-tier service model that auditors will flag.

Phased approach is standard. No peer launched Tier 1 and Tier 2 simultaneously. The pattern is: prove Tier 1 execution → define Tier 2 scope → enable and validate → expand. Attempting both at once increases risk without accelerating timeline.

What If...

What if customers never felt the handoff? In high-stakes financial services, trust erodes the moment a customer senses they're being passed around. Schwab and Fidelity obsess over "warm transfers" — the receiving advocate already has context, the customer doesn't repeat themselves, resolution continues seamlessly. The differentiator isn't speed. It's continuity. When tool access, knowledge coverage, and escalation design are built for continuity, the customer experiences one conversation — not a series of handoffs. That's the gap between transactional support and relationship-driven support. Brokerage customers are making financial decisions. They need to trust the person on the other end knows their situation and can act on it.

What if advocates felt equipped, not exposed? The difference between confident advocates and anxious ones isn't tenure — it's whether they have what they need in the moment. Vanguard's BPO model obsesses over "decision support at the point of need": knowledge articles structured as decision trees, escalation rules that remove ambiguity, and real-time access to SMEs when edge cases surface. Advocates don't feel exposed on regulated work when they know exactly what they can handle and exactly when to escalate. That confidence changes how they show up for customers. It changes tone, pace, and resolution quality. Advocate obsession means designing systems that make them feel capable, not vulnerable.

What if this unlocked strategic capacity, not just headcount? The business case for BPO is usually cost. But the strategic case is focus. When Tier 2 moves to FE at the same quality bar, internal advocates aren't just freed up — they're freed up to work on the problems only they can solve. Complex fraud investigations. Regulatory edge cases that require deep institutional knowledge. Product feedback loops that shape what gets built. That's not a cost play. That's a capability play. The business wins when high-skill internal advocates stop doing work that well-enabled partners can handle and start doing work that moves the business forward.

What if we designed for the customer who never calls? The best CX isn't fast support — it's support you don't need. Stripe and Square obsess over deflection: self-service that actually works, proactive notifications that answer questions before they're asked, and support experiences so clear that customers don't have to follow up. If this migration is designed well — knowledge articles that customers can find, advocates who resolve on first contact, and quality that holds under complexity — fewer customers call back. That's the ultimate differentiator. Not how fast we answer. How rarely they need us to.
