Migration

Launch Plan

Overall Status: Green — Ready for Launch
Launch can proceed within regulatory timeline. No blockers. Elevated operational risk due to compressed enablement window and lack of advocate testing. Mitigations in place through job aids, targeted enablement, and post-launch monitoring.
Background
PMD dispute intake currently uses manual QuickTexts with free-form data capture, manual routing, and heavy reliance on advocate judgment. This creates inconsistent Notice of Error capture and limited auditability. Because PMD disputes qualify as Electronic Funds Transfers under Regulation E, intake must meet regulatory requirements for documentation, consistency, and auditability. This migration moves PMD intake into the existing Disputes platform. Hard regulatory deadline March 31, 2026, driven by Reg E compliance and MAP consent order commitments.
What's Changing
Advocates move from manual QT-driven intake to guided Toolbox "Submit Dispute" experience. New flow: disclosure acknowledgment, 6 structured error reason categories, store confirmation gate, optional evidence upload, transaction lookup support. Routing becomes automatic. Disputes agents continue to own investigation and resolution. Existing Reg E investigation standards and SLAs unchanged.

Industry Worldview

How leading fintech companies approach dispute platform migrations and Reg E compliance.

Industry Patterns

Structured dispute intake is table stakes for regulated financial services. Stripe, Square, Coinbase, and Robinhood all migrated from manual dispute intake to platform-based flows to meet regulatory requirements. The pattern is consistent: manual workflows create compliance risk through inconsistent data capture and audit trail fragmentation. Platform-based intake standardizes Notice of Error capture, automates routing, and creates audit trails regulators can validate.

The most common failure mode is launching without advocate testing. When advocates encounter new dispute flows for the first time in production, confusion spikes and intake errors increase. The mitigation is always the same: comprehensive job aids, clear escalation paths, and real-time monitoring to catch friction early.

Compressed timelines driven by regulatory deadlines are standard. Consent orders create hard deadlines that cannot slip. The successful pattern: accept the timeline risk, build strong mitigations, and establish clear escalation paths for launch week.

What If...

What if advocates felt equipped, not exposed? The difference between confident advocates and anxious ones is whether they have what they need in the moment. Job aids structured as decision trees, escalation rules that remove ambiguity, and real-time access to support when edge cases surface. Advocates do not feel exposed on regulated work when they know exactly what they can handle and exactly when to escalate.

What if customers never felt the change? The best migrations are invisible. When tooling is intuitive, guidance is clear, and advocates are confident, customers experience continuity. The intake mechanism changes, but the customer experience does not degrade.

What if this unlocked strategic capacity? Structured intake reduces manual work, eliminates routing errors, and creates audit trails automatically. Advocates spend less time on data entry and more time on customer support. Disputes teams receive clean, complete intake data and spend less time on rework. That is not just compliance. That is operational leverage.
